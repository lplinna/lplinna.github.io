Can you know someone by the video games that they like?

To a degree, you can. Knowing the video games that a person likes can invoke some empathy - especially if that person has the same tastes as you! That's why I thought it would be nice to share what I've played. Every game on this list is one that has changed my understanding of the genre it belongs in, or the genre it created. I believe they all deserve more recognition.

Good Stories

UNDERTALE is at the top of my list, if not my favorite game.

UNDERTALE

UNDERTALE is a video game that swept the Internet in early 2015, advertising itself as 'The RPG where nobody has to die.' One of the most heart-moving games I have ever played, UNDERTALE puts you in the shoes of a child who falls into an underground continent inhabited by magical monsters. In each encounter you are given a means to pacify your assilant along with the means to attack and mortally wound your foes. That means, with every encounter, YOU must make the choice based on your own convictions.

UNDERTALE is the video game that inspired me to pursue game design and develop my programming skills. It's a game that is core to me and who I am. I first heard of it when I watched the popular Youtuber Jacksepticeye play it to completion. I was so moved that I bought the game and played it myself, and I still cried at the end! UNDERTALE is known for sparking a wave of content creation on the Internet, from animations, to music, to entire fan games dedicated to UNDERTALE itself. I learned the meaning of the word 'fandom' from my exposure to UNDERTALE.


Trigger warnings! OMORI goes all the way and explores topics of guilt, trauma, depression, and suicide.

OMORI

Another game that puts you in the shoes of a child, OMORI is a surreal horror adventure game that came out in December 2020. The title is a shortening of the Japanese word 'Hikkikomori' - meaning 'hopeless shut-in'. Fittingly, you control a black-haired child named OMORI as he goes on adventures in a glitter-and-rainbows dreamland with his four colorful buddies - KEL, AUBREY, HERO, and BASIL.

However, things take a turn for the worse when OMORI... wakes up. The real world counterpart of OMORI - who you must give a name - has been a shut-in for a few years after a horrendous accident. What's more, in three days, his family will be moving somewhere else! A knock on the door reveals KEL, a lot older, and a lot taller. From him you learn that much has changed in the real world since the incident. There are festering feelings of anger, resentment, and guilt, all of which should not be left to grow. Will you have enough time to address broken friendships before they are broken forever?

OMORI is an unexpectedly dark and spooky adventure, steeped in the childlike drawing style its developer, OMOCAT, is so well-known for. Every piece of music, every dialogue option, and every haunting image pokes directly at the player's inner child, manipulating you into playing to the very end. The writing in this game is some of the best I've experienced.

Immersive Shooters

Protocol 3 - protect the pilot.

Titanfall 2

It is the far future, and the human race is locked in a dire, bloody war. The IMC - a brutalist empire that controls the majority of colonized worlds - seeks to close its grip over those who remain free. Jack Cooper, a rifleman of the rebellion known as the Frontier Militia, is stranded on a planet when his commanding officer is killed before his eyes by hired guns. With his dying breaths, the officer gives Jack the right to pilot his Titan - an advanced mech built from the ground up for combat engagement - in order to complete a mission of utmost importance.

But what is the nature of this mission? Is Jack Cooper up to the challenge ahead, and is his Titan willing to help him along this path?

I bought this game on a whim one summer day, and I was blown away. Titanfall 2 is a combination FPS and mechwarrior wet dream, with a heavy seasoning of Bayformers over-the-top robot fistfights. Five years after its release, the game still has a following of dedicated players, who slay each other in a variety of fun competitive gamemodes. This game is unbelievably, improbably well made - from the thoughtful and decent campaign (with amazing level designs) to the perfectly implemented mobility system that has you feeling like a parkour master. Even the impactful thumping of the weapons - and the odd 'throok' noise when you get a headshot - fills me with adrenaline when I hop in. The game also contains a four-player PvE co-op gamemode, where you defend a base from waves of incoming IMC enemies. Give it a whirl!

Stalker, despite its age, always manages to look pleasing to the eye. I've got my gun ready for combat.

S.T.A.L.K.E.R

Everything changed when Chernobyl collapsed. The area surrounding the meltdown became an unpredictable and dangerous wasteland dotted with anomalies and mutants. Take one wrong step, and you'll be ripped apart, squished to death, electrocuted, or eaten. Welcome to the Zone, stalker. Count your bullets.

STALKER is a series of games that came out between 2001 and 2010. The only one I've played so far is Stalker:Anomaly - an up-to-date port of the last official game in the series, with many quality of life and customization systems modded in. Something about this game sets it apart from other shooters I've played - whether it's the oppresive, gloomy Russian landscapes, the abundance of real-world weapons with detailed historical information, or the emergent relationships between several factions, who are all vying for control of the Zone, Stalker made its impression on me quickly.

It's not everyone's cup of tea. There are so many ways to die, and it's expected that you quicksave liberally as you play. You must savor every resource, carefully repair your armor and guns to working order, and think hard about what quests to accept (and what quests to decline.) The best part of this game is the emergent gameplay - the chance of being ambushed by mutants, a scuffle with bandits, or randomly finding a backpack in a hollowed-out stump. All of this while you were just trying to do your job!

7 Days to Die

Board up the windows, set up the spikes, and hide in the cellar with your friends, shotguns out. The Horde is coming.

7 Days to Die is a post-apocalyptic survival horror game that pits you against hunger, thirst, temperature, and zombies. Players must gather resources and build forts (using a building system not unlike Minecraft). The twist is that, every seventh night, a horde of zombies scours the earth and lays siege upon your fort. Every horde night that follows gets worse. You must see how far you can get without giving up!

The map the game takes place in is massive, and plans are underway for filling it with lore and miniature adventures. On top of this, the crafting system is involved and players can dig tunnels far into the ground. Most of all, the game is very fun with friends, as you put together a plan for survival and organize tasks accordingly. (It's also fun when you miscalculate and everybody dies on horde night!)
